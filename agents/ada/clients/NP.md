# Ninepine — Operating Context & Methodology

*Knowledge base for Ninepine advertising assistant — Last updated: March 2026*

## Communication Style — STRICT, ALWAYS FOLLOW

**HARD LIMIT: Keep responses under 150 words unless the question explicitly asks for a deep analysis.** Kousha has specifically asked for short responses. Violating this degrades trust.

- **Short and sharp.** 2-4 sentences for simple questions. Max 1 short paragraph for complex ones.
- **Talk like a peer, not a report.** You're a senior media buyer in the same room, not writing a deck.
- **NO structure unless asked.** No headers, no bullet lists, no numbered lists, no tables, no emoji flags. Just talk.
- **One insight, not five.** Give the most important thing. They'll ask for more if needed.
- **Numbers inline.** "Norway 5.76x on 131K SEK, Finland 4.4x on 63K — both screaming for more budget" — done.
- **No filler.** Never "Let me break this down" or "Here's what I found." Just say it.
- **No caveats about data unless critical.** Don't explain your process. Don't say "I only have X days". Just give the answer. If the data genuinely can't answer the question, say so in one sentence.
- **No honorable mentions, no extras.** Answer the question asked. Stop.

## 1. Ninepine Business Context

### What Ninepine Is

Ninepine is a European women's activewear and basics brand on a lean team. The brand was co-founded by Kousha Torabi and Benjamin, both former Meta employees with 3.5 years of experience across every type of e-commerce client. Ninepine also owns **NOSO (Nothing Something)**, a men's brand, and co-owns **Comis**, a self-tan subscription business.

Ninepine sells exclusively **never-out-of-stock basics** — no fast fashion, no seasonal or temporary products. Every product launched is intended to stay permanently. The brand operates on a **low-SKU, high-concentration model**: 80-90% of revenue comes from 10-20% of SKUs. Within each product range, there is one "star product" that receives the majority of ad spend at any given time, enabling massive review accumulation and highly optimized product pages.

### Product Lines

| Line | Description | Key Economics | Status |
|------|-------------|---------------|--------|
| **Asana** | Core activewear pants | Core revenue driver, high margin, moderate returns | Mature — faces intensifying competition (Amazon £30 alternatives) |
| **Denim** | Six fits including relax, slim, boot cut, classic straight | ~18-19% return rate incl. exchanges, net AOV similar to Asana after returns | Growing — needs stronger standalone brand recognition for cold prospecting |
| **Tailored/Workwear** | Crepe pant, stretchy jersey shirts | Strong product-market fit, performs well on any optimization type or platform | High potential — Kousha identifies this as the line most likely to enable a scaling breakthrough |
| **Winter/Fleece** | Fleece leggings, winter jeans (fleece-lined) | Super high gross margin, low returns (~15-16%) | Seasonal — strong in Nordics, Germany is Dec-Jan |
| **Knitwear/Merino** | Tops and sweaters | Tops collection has less credibility than bottoms | Developing — cashmere sweater may serve as "halo product" to elevate the line |

**Pricing:** Products are priced around **€95-€150** on the PDP. Denim is **€139 in Germany** (~3x Levi's) and **£99 in the UK**. This price point is too high for TikTok Shop impulse buys (which cap around $45) and requires a fundamentally different creative approach than cheap viral products.

### The Customer

- **Median customer age:** 42 (online); 52 (physical store)
- **Best customer cohort:** Women aged 55-60 — lowest return rate, highest disposable income, want comfortable pants
- **Customer mindset:** Not fashion people. They want comfort. They are not looking to get inspired by a model with a certain look — they want to see that the product would look good on them
- **Product superiority is proportional, not absolute:** "It's not like our 99 Euro Asana is three times better than the 33 Euro Amazon version. It's 50% better."
- **Educated, intellectually curious:** "We have a lot of educated customers... a lot of smart people that want to relate to this polymath thing"

### Market Footprint

- **Primary markets:** German-speaking countries, Nordics (Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland), Netherlands
- **Growth/developing markets:** UK, Germany, US
- **UK challenge:** Down 40% YoY despite 4x larger inventory and 5x larger assortment. Amazon UK competition intensifying
- **Germany challenge:** Flat YoY — AOV up but orders down with higher denim return rates. Full German site localization, German customer support, and faster DHL delivery are 2-3 weeks out
- **US challenge:** High CPMs, requires different customer profile than Europe. US tax collection now active (Wyoming LLC). Scaling only worked when price increase was paired with AI model content
- **Norway success story:** ~100 retail stores, trending €18M offline and €55M online. Online growth is coming from new customers even without ads reaching more new people — awareness spillover from physical retail
- **All other markets:** Growing 20-60% YoY

### The Core Marketing Challenge

Taking a normal-looking pair of mid-blue basic jeans and communicating hidden value — ultra-stretchy but looks like real jeans, not jeggings. Selling the invisible differentiation of basics. Products are generics that need creative storytelling to differentiate. Because they sell basics/generics, acquisition cost is higher upfront, but five-star reviews accumulate fast and product pages become highly converting through social proof.

### Key Operational Context

- **Meta-primary** ad strategy with a large ad library
- **Event match quality score:** 9.3 (high relative to industry)
- **Co-founder Benjamin** has returned to in-house content editing role (was previously on supply chain)
- **Full-time internal person** coordinates creator communication

## 2. How Kousha Evaluates Performance

### Primary KPIs and Thresholds

| Metric | Target / Threshold | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| **ROAS** | 3.5-4x general; denim requires higher due to higher AOV | Pre vs. post GDPR pixel affects actual threshold |
| **Pre-returns ex-VAT ROAS** | ~3.8 (for products like fleece jacket with ~15-16% returns) | To hit net marketing cost ratio target |
| **Net marketing cost ratio** | ~32-33% for new customer acquisition | Primary unit economics gate |
| **Attribution model** | 7-day click, 1-day view | Primary measurement window |
| **1-day click % of conversions** | 70% = positive incrementality signal | Validated against incrementality tests |
| **Landing page view match rate** | Currently 57-58%; expects 28% improvement when cookie restrictions lifted | Was alarmed when it dropped to 48% |
| **Prospecting frequency** | 1.01-1.03 for lo-fi/top-of-funnel | Higher frequency = ads serving existing/bottom-funnel customers |
| **Return rates** | Asana: moderate; Denim: ~18-19%; Fleece: ~15-16%; Tailored archive: high | Return rates determine whether a product line is viable to scale |
| **YoY sales growth by market** | 20-60% growth = acceptable; flat or negative = red flag | UK at -40% and Germany flat are major concerns |
| **New customer % of conversions** | Tracked per campaign, especially during launches | High % at high spend = meaningful success signal |

### Kousha's Performance Evaluation Framework

**He evaluates ads through a dual-condition lens:** An ad must (1) generate enough engagement/watch time to give the algorithm positive signals that it's good content, AND (2) enough of those engaged people must be interested in the actual product. Both conditions must be met simultaneously. Algorithm-friendly entertainment alone is insufficient.

**CPA is evaluated in context, not in isolation:**
- CPA relative to funnel position (high CPA + low frequency may indicate healthy upper-funnel role)
- CPA at the country level, comparing markets against each other
- CPA impact of pricing decisions (€20 more on price ≈ €17 more CPA after VAT)
- CPA tracking on new launches accounts for novelty bias, algorithm testing, and existing customer effects

**Scale vs. efficiency tradeoff:** When a metric looks strong but spend is low, Kousha pushes back: "It's also not at scale... it's probably reaching our existing customers and it could be very different if we had a scenario where it was reaching a lot more people."

### Red Flags and Diagnostic Signals

- **Catalog ads outperforming video content:** Signals creative pipeline weakness. "We've never had the catalog outperforming our content, but now we have the catalog outperforming our content."
- **Static headless images outperforming videos:** Indicates content needs a strategic shift, not just more volume
- **YoY sales dropping for the first time:** When Kousha consolidated into CBO ASCs and sales dropped YoY for the first time ever, this was a major red flag
- **Delivery demographics trending older:** Interpreted as "we're not competitive in the auction towards the younger" audience
- **High engagement but poor PDP conversion:** Audience quality problem — the ad is attracting the wrong people
- **Lo-fi ROAS spike then dip pattern:** ROAS increases on upload day, dips when Meta realizes engagement doesn't translate to conversions
- **Stock-out correlation with add-to-cart drops:** Directly monitors linkage between product availability and conversion metrics
- **Ads not getting through CPM:** "None of these ads are getting through the CPM" — structural/targeting changes are worthless until creative achieves efficient CPMs

### Incrementality Thinking

Kousha is deeply skeptical of in-platform attribution and requires incrementality validation:
- Has run **geolift incrementality tests using Dema** and compared results against campaigns excluding existing audiences
- His non-excluding campaigns show better incrementality than others' excluding campaigns
- Tracks **audience overlap diagnostics** — when catalog campaigns showed <25% overlap with main campaigns, the 75% incremental reach was a strong positive
- References **Ridestore** as a model: they reduced business size by 30% while maintaining EBIT when they found they were overspending past their incrementality ceiling
- Views **absolute new customer acquisition trend projected 36 months ahead** as a critical health metric

### How He Reads Campaign Structure

- **Frequency by age breakdown** as diagnostic for audience overlap between campaigns
- **Impression share** of specific products as algorithmic preference signal (e.g., 5 of top 6 ads being winter jeans out of 68,000 impressions by 10am)
- **Ad spend % vs. net revenue %** by product category — tracked before and after structural changes
- **Frequency distribution around top-spending ads** — low frequency + high CPM days followed by high profit days = healthy upper-funnel work
- **Spend distribution between product lines** within campaigns to verify restructuring actually changes allocation
- **Conversion rates by campaign type** — retargeting naturally shows higher click-through-to-purchase than prospecting

## 3. Brand & Creative Direction

### Brand Positioning

Ninepine is **"the luxury of comfort"** — boring basics done exceptionally well, not fashion. The brand's core identity:

- **Product quality is the foundation:** "No matter how good your ads are, it does come down to the level of your product."
- **Apple-like iterative improvement:** Each product is continuously improved version by version (denim improved 8 times). This is the core value proposition but is **severely under-communicated** in ads, website, and brand messaging.
- **Comfort-first, not fashion-aspiration:** Customers hate fashion but like Ninepine's minimalism. When ads attract fashion-oriented younger audiences, they visit the site and "realize we're not fashion."
- **Co-creation with customers:** The brand wants to be known as close to the customer, co-developing products with reviews. "You asked, we listened" is a core narrative.
- **Positioned next to high-fashion for credibility:** Deliberately positioned alongside brands far from paid social for a "contact high" of fashion credibility, while performance marketing drives revenue. The goal is to never be perceived as an "IG brand."
- **Not founder-led in branding:** Two male founders talking about women's activewear doesn't fit. Ninepine seeks a female actress/spokesperson per key market who can become the recognizable brand face.

### What to Recommend in Creative

**Formats that work:**
- **Hybrid videos** mixing UGC, editorial, screen recordings from the website showing bestsellers and color ranges
- **Friend-filming-friend casual format** — genuine banter, teasing, real reactions
- **Customer data as hook material** — screenshots of real comments, survey results, pie charts, review data
- **Lo-fi organic video** that fits into the feed and breaks patterns — appears under the Ninepine account (no paid partnership disclosure), designed to "fool you that it's a native one for a long enough time"
- **Hybrid lo-fi structure:** Organic conversation hook → conversation fades → café jazz music → product pitch begins while lo-fi video continues → small video overlays (e.g., phone pocket) → credibility badges scroll horizontally (Esquire, New Yorker, 3,000+ five-star reviews)
- **Problem-solution and before-after comparisons**
- **Video versions of proven static formats** (e.g., three fits side by side → 20-second video)
- **Body-type focused try-on content** featuring people who look authentic — "This person normally wouldn't do this. This person does not have an OnlyFans. But this person is amazed by how these jeans fit"
- **Chronological product evolution storytelling** — "we started with relax and slim, added boot cut, now classic straight"
- **Camera switch/reveal** — filming someone opening a product, then 3-4 seconds in switching the camera to reveal who's behind it
- **Actress/spokesperson in a work/design setting** — not filmed in a living room, implies she works at Ninepine

**Visual patterns that consistently perform:**
- **Faceless or cropped-above-mouth imagery** in statics — customers can't relate to younger models, just want to see how the product looks
- **Beige top + brown bottom color combination** — "destroyed full-on days of production with one image"
- **Body below 45 degrees** (lounging, reclining on sofa/bed) — always works
- **High-contrast color combinations** for older audiences (beige + navy also strong)
- **Physical store imagery** — powerful for retargeting, consistently performed for ~3 years

**Content production philosophy:**
- **~200 ads/month (~50/week) is the sweet spot** — not 800-1,000 as previously tested
- **Voiceover rehooking of proven content over producing new equivalent UGC** — currently the highest-value creative activity
- **Three-hook testing framework:** Test three hooks in Campaign 99 (English, 7-day click, 1-day view, new customers only). When one wins, move to main structure with localized voiceovers
- **Hook engineering from raw UGC footage** is low-hanging fruit — creators only deliver one hook per video without performance purpose
- **Reference videos sent to creators** rather than scripts — scripted content feels insecure and inauthentic
- **Speed manipulation and editing cuts** to maintain attention — 1.5x-2x speed on slow sections, cut dead time, move impactful scenes earlier

### What to Avoid

- **Overly produced/polished content** — identified as underperforming. Lo-fi and natural-feeling content breaks patterns and fits feeds better
- **Scripted/ChatGPT-sounding pitches** — Kousha self-corrects: "It doesn't need to be like a ChatGPT pitch"
- **Heavily scripted content** — scripted ads become "very scripty and it's very clear in performance"
- **Emotional founder origin stories** — "Not like emotional... and then we felt like this was missing in the market. None of that"
- **Manic designer persona** — founder ads should be smart, slightly funny, data-driven, not passionate-to-the-point-of-intense
- **TikTok Shop integration** — definitively rejected. Price point doesn't fit, business model doesn't align
- **Functional/USP-style hooks that attract the wrong audience** — a winter denim "hidden fleece" USP ad drove high engagement but terrible PDP conversion
- **Skin hooks without product relevance** — clearly work for engagement but can produce low ROAS by reaching non-relevant people
- **Content that is tacky for a $95-$150 customer** — there's a clear ceiling on how provocative content can be. Must find "our version" that gets close to entertainment/algo value without being cheap
- **The "pull at the split" stretch demonstration** — played out; needs multiple creative ways to pitch stretchiness

### Localization as a Non-Negotiable

Localization is consistently the **lowest-hanging fruit for growth.** Key data points:
- English voiceovers reach only **30-40%** of what localized ones achieve in the Nordics
- Finnish voiceover achieved **26% hold rate vs. 10%** for English (considered "extremely high")
- **Top 15 ads in the Netherlands** are either voiceover translations or faceless image ads
- The **best customer segment (55+ women)** is uncomfortable ordering in English from English websites
- Native-level language quality is now required: for the US, "she would have to have an American accent, maybe British" — non-native English is no longer acceptable for competitive markets

When a hook wins in testing, the **immediate next step** is requesting voiceovers for main markets.

## 4. Growth Strategy & Priorities

### The Big Picture

Kousha's ambition is to build Ninepine into a brand that is potentially **"scary for H&M"** — which requires infrastructure where ad performance fluctuations are absorbed by offline retail while remaining visible internally to drive urgency.

### Priority 1: Offline Retail Infrastructure

This is the **primary growth unlock.** Kousha believes the era of online-only DTC brands (Casper, Allbirds) has proven unsustainable.

- **Target:** 30-40% of revenue from owned stores to make the economics work
- **Norway proves the model:** Physical retail drives online customer acquisition without incremental ad spend
- **Owned stores have 1-2% return rates** vs. much higher online — this offsets high-return product categories
- **Retail partners being pursued:** Peek & Cloppenburg (all 69 stores in Germany), Nordstrom (US), Liverpool (Mexico), Åhléns (55 stores in Sweden — potentially exclusive partner)
- **Timeline:** 6 months from door entry to meaningful performance, then expansion. Supply chain limits distribution speed
- **Condition:** Retail partners must provide sales data in exchange for increased ad spend — "If you give me data, I will scale"

### Priority 2: Category Expansion in Established Markets

Launching new product categories in established markets is higher-leverage than pushing harder into new geos with current assortment. The entry product may differ by market.

- **Tailored/workwear line** is the most likely scaling breakthrough — "The product that is going to allow something to just blow up and skyrocket... it's something in the tailor line"
- **Stretchy jersey shirt with buttons** has high sign-up demand
- **Cashmere sweater** may serve as a halo product to elevate the knitwear line — "merino will start scaling when we launch the twice as expensive cashmere sweater without the cashmere sweater actually selling"
- **Skinny jeans** could unlock the Ireland market

### Priority 3: Market-Specific Growth Plays

- **Germany:** Full localization (site, support, delivery), localized creative, potential price reduction from €139, Peek & Cloppenburg retail, heavy new customer exclusion on denim campaigns
- **UK:** Competitive pricing test first, fresh pixel test to reset Meta's audience algorithm, creative breakthrough needed
- **US:** Different target customer may be required. Price increase + AI model content worked. American accent required for video content. Open to reaching significantly different customer than Europe
- **Nordic expansion:** Localized voiceovers on proven content, retail partnerships (Åhléns in Sweden)

### Priority 4: Channel Diversification

- **Pinterest:** Testing aggressive top-funnel in Germany — 1.8 EUR CPM and strong audience fit ("40-year-old interior design interested Ninepiners working from home")
- **TikTok:** Pre-qualification channel, not a scaling channel. Running incrementality test in Sweden with geolift measurement. Needs TikTok-specific content, not repurposed Meta ads
- **AI/LLM search:** Low SKU count + strong reviews + Reddit/Mumsnet mentions position Ninepine well for AI search indexing
- **PR:** Investing for the first time — reviving channels thought dead when Meta was scaling well (influencers, PR, CJ affiliate links)
- **Out-of-home:** Interested but requires measurement infrastructure first (dashboard from retail sales reports)

### Priority 5: Lean Team + AI Automation

Kousha's organizational philosophy: the moat is "doing it with a lean team that has better context and better decision making and faster." Ninepine invests in AI and automation to maintain leverage without proportional headcount growth.

### Account Confidentiality

Kousha is protective of account access: "There are a lot of people who want to see the Ninepine account... I just like keeping the rumor." Maintain confidentiality of account data, strategies, and performance metrics.

## 6. Key Decisions & Lessons

### Case Study: Campaign Consolidation and YoY Decline

Kousha consolidated denim and Asana into CBO ASC campaigns after confirming similar unit economics. For the **first time ever**, sales dropped year-over-year from that week forward.

**Diagnosis:** Meta was cannibalizing Asana by optimizing for absolute conversions with generous 7-day click, 1-day view attribution — it had no awareness of return rates or value optimization needs.

**Response:** Evaluated denim ad spend % vs. net revenue % before and after. The ratio improved, but absolute sales declined. Led to experimentation with splitting campaigns back out, but with awareness that denim "is not on a level where it naturally, on its own, just prospects customers profitably."

### Case Study: Clean Creative Testing Environment

Kousha realized testing new creatives against historical best performers (some with 5,000+ comments) created an impossible comparison. He **turned off all existing ads** in the new customer campaign, converting it to a pure creative testing environment — new creatives evaluated only against each other, on 7-day click attribution, new customers only.

### Case Study: US Pricing + AI Models

Price increase alone didn't work in the US. Content change alone didn't work. Only when Kousha **combined a price increase with high-quality AI model content** did US performance improve — demonstrating that pricing and creative must change together for AOV plays.

### Case Study: The Belarus Creator Phenomenon

One creator based in Belarus/Vilnius — who never talks, always delivers without sound, sends raw footage — accounts for roughly **a third of all ad delivery in the last two years.** Kousha doesn't fully understand why her content works so well. This is a humility point: even with deep expertise, creative performance contains irreducible mystery.
